How do I interpret simulation results? ‚ñº

Key metrics to focus on:

  • Utilisation Rate: Percentage of time the system is busy. 85%+ indicates high usage, 95%+ approaching capacity
  • Queue Length (90th percentile): Maximum expected queue size 90% of the time
  • Average Wait Time: Time vehicles spend waiting before being served
  • Delay Probability: Percentage of vehicles that must wait rather than being served immediately
  • Blocking Rate: Percentage of vehicles turned away when system is full
Pro tip: Use percentiles (90th, 95th) for capacity planning as they show peak demand scenarios, not just averages.

What are "seeds" and how many should I use? ‚ñº

Seeds are independent simulation runs that ensure statistical reliability. Each seed uses different random numbers to model vehicle arrivals and service times.

  • 100 seeds: Quick analysis, suitable for initial estimates
  • 500+ seeds: Higher precision for design decisions
  • 1000+ seeds: Critical studies requiring maximum accuracy

More seeds provide more accurate results but require longer computation time. The system shows a sample size calculator to help determine the optimal number.

How do I choose the right arrival rate and service time? ‚ñº

Arrival Rate (vehicles per hour):

  • shopping centre peak: 200-400 cars/hr
  • Office building: 100-250 cars/hr
  • Boom gate average: 30-60 cars/hr
  • School pickup: 50-150 cars/hr during dismissal

Service Time (seconds per vehicle):

  • Boom gate: 15-20 seconds
  • Parking validation: 25-35 seconds
  • Toll booth: 20-25 seconds
  • Car lift (floor-to-floor): 20-40 seconds
Data collection tip: Observe your specific site during peak periods to get accurate measurements for your scenario.

What does "minimum headway" mean? ‚ñº

Minimum headway is the minimum time interval between consecutive vehicles, ensuring realistic traffic constraints and safety requirements.

  • Highway following distance: 1.5-2 seconds
  • Parking entrance safety gap: 3-4 seconds
  • Boom gate processing gap: 2-3 seconds
  • School pickup safety buffer: 4-5 seconds

This parameter prevents unrealistic scenarios where vehicles arrive too close together to be safely processed.

What's the difference between different confidence levels? ‚ñº

Confidence levels indicate how certain you can be that results fall within the margin of error:

  • 95% confidence (Z=1.96): Standard for most engineering applications
  • 99% confidence (Z=2.576): Higher precision for critical infrastructure
  • 99.9% confidence (Z=3.291): Maximum precision for safety-critical systems

Higher confidence levels require more simulation runs to achieve the same margin of error, but provide greater statistical certainty.

How do I design compliant ramps with the Ramp Designer? ‚ñº

The Ramp Designer creates vehicle ramps with proper gradients and compliance annotations:

  • Gradient input: Enter as percentage (8%) or ratio (1:12)
  • RL (Reduced Level): Elevation relative to sea level datum
  • Chainage: Horizontal distance along the ramp alignment
  • Transition Length: Distance over which gradient changes occur
Output includes: Technical drawings, CAD-ready exports, gradient compliance checks, and construction annotations.

What does "FCFS" mean and when is it used? ‚ñº

FCFS (First Come First Served) is a queue discipline where vehicles are served in the order they arrive, regardless of origin or destination.

Common applications:

  • Toll booths and payment gates
  • Most parking facility entrances
  • Drive-through services
  • Single-lane traffic control

This is the most fair and common queuing method, contrasting with priority schemes that might serve certain vehicle types first.

How are percentiles different from averages? ‚ñº

Percentiles show the value below which a certain percentage of observations fall, giving insight into system performance under varying conditions:

  • Average queue length: 3.2 vehicles
  • 90th percentile queue: 8 vehicles (queue exceeds 8 cars only 10% of time)
  • 95th percentile wait time: 180 seconds (only 5% wait longer than 3 minutes)
Why percentiles matter: They help you plan for peak demand scenarios, not just typical conditions. Design capacity should handle 90th-95th percentile demands.

How long do I stay logged in? ‚ñº

Your login session lasts for 24 hours by default. After this time, you'll need to log in again for security purposes.

The system will automatically log you out if:

  • Your session expires (24 hours)
  • You manually click "Logout"
  • You clear your browser's local storage

Your work is automatically saved during simulations, so you won't lose progress if your session expires.

Can I save and export my simulation results? ‚ñº

Yes, all simulation results can be saved and exported in multiple formats:

  • PDF reports: Complete analysis with charts and recommendations
  • CSV data: Raw simulation data for further analysis
  • CAD exports: Technical drawings (Ramp Designer)
  • Summary tables: Key metrics and performance indicators
Results remain accessible during your session. For permanent storage, download the reports before logging out.

What should I do if a simulation takes too long? ‚ñº

Simulation time depends on complexity and number of seeds:

  • Reduce seeds temporarily: Use 100-200 seeds for initial testing
  • Check parameters: Very high arrival rates or complex configurations take longer
  • Monitor progress: The progress bar shows estimated completion time
  • Browser issues: Refresh the page if progress stalls

Most simulations complete within 5-30 seconds. If a simulation exceeds 2 minutes, check your input parameters or contact support.

Traffic Engineering Technical Terms

Comprehensive definitions of technical terms, parameters, and metrics used throughout our simulation and design tools.

Arrival Rate Input/Output
Units: vehicles/hour (veh/hr)
The average rate at which vehicles arrive at the entry to a system, or if there is a queue, then the arrival to the back of the queue. For monte-carlo simulations this is expressed separately as the input arrival rate and output arrival rate for model validation purposes.
Examples: Child Care Centre: 35 veh/hr, Boom gate: 30 veh/hr average, Car lift: 15 veh/hr
Average Occupancy Output
Units: spaces
The mean number of vehicles present in a car park or facility at any given time during the analysis period. Can be expressed in absolute terms or percentage.
Examples: 50 space car park with avg occupancy of 35 = 70% average utilisation
Blocking Rate Output
Units: percentage (%)
The percentage of arriving vehicles which arrive to the system or back of queue and there is insufficient queueing capacity. That vehicle is then blocked from both queueing and being serviced. Those vehicles are not included in queueing statistics.
Examples: 5% blocking = 1 in 20 vehicles turned away
Capacity Input
Units: vehicles or spaces
The maximum number of vehicles that can be accommodated in the system simultaneously. May refer to parking spaces, service bays, lift capacity, or road segments.
Examples: Carpark: 500 spaces, Car lift: 1 vehicle at a time, Mechanical parking: 120 vehicles, Queue Bay: 3 vehicles
Chainage Design
Units: metres (m) or millimetres (mm)
The cumulative longitudinal distance along a roadway or ramp from a reference starting point. Used to locate specific points along the alignment. Often prefixed with 'CH', 'CL', or 'ST'.
Examples: CH50 = 50m from start, CH1.200 = 1200mm from start, Starting chainage: 0.000m
Confidence Level Analysis
Units: percentage (%)
The statistical confidence that simulation results fall within the specified margin of error. Higher confidence levels require more simulation runs (seeds).
Examples: 95% confidence (Z=1.96), 99% confidence (Z=2.576), 99.9% confidence (Z=3.291)
Delay Probability Output
Units: percentage (%)
The probability that an arriving vehicle will have to wait in queue at all, rather than being served immediately.
Examples: 25% = 1 in 4 vehicles wait at all
Door Opening/Closing Time Input
Units: seconds (s)
The average time for lift or mechanical system doors to fully open or close. Critical component of total service time in vertical transportation systems.
Examples: Car lift doors: 8 seconds, Parking lift: 5 seconds
Exponential Distribution Analysis
Units: N/A (statistical distribution)
A probability distribution to transform a uniform random variable into a continuous distribution for a poisson event. Can be used for either the continuous gap between events, or the duration of events. This distribution is memoryless where past events don't affect future probabilities. Exponential distributions have standard deviation equal to the mean.
Examples: Random arrivals at a carpark, Variable service times at boom gate
FCFS (First Come First Served) Method
Units: N/A
A servicing model where vehicles are served in the order they arrive to the back of any queue, regardless of their origin or destination. This combines multiple queues into a single theoretical arrival queue independent of queue length of each individual queue. In practice this implies uniform patience of drivers and 'giving way' to other queued vehicles which may have arrived earlier. This queue model is most appropriate where queues are within sight distance and shorter, such as 20m long ramps with low utilisation rates. Drivers are typically more selfish and less fair in practice and breakdown is likely in large or congested systems.
Examples: Two way passing with one-way segment of 30m
Floor-to-Floor Time Input
Units: seconds (s)
The time required to travel between adjacent floors in a lift or elevator system, including acceleration, travel, and deceleration phases. Key parameter for vertical transportation analysis.
Examples: Standard car lift: 20-30 seconds, High-speed lift: 10-15 seconds, Mechanical parking: 25 seconds
Grade / Gradient Design
Units: percentage (%) or ratio (1:X)
The slope of a ramp or roadway, expressed as either a percentage (rise/run × 100) or a ratio (1:X where X is horizontal distance per unit rise). Critical for vehicle accessibility and safety.
Examples: 8% grade = 8m rise per 100m run, 1:12 ratio = 1m rise per 12m horizontal, Max car park ramp: 15-20%
Headway Input
Units: seconds (s)
The minimum time interval between consecutive vehicle arrivals or departures. Used to model realistic traffic constraints, safety requirements, and capacity limitations.
Examples: Highway: 1.5s following distance, Parking entrance: 3s safety gap, School pickup: 10s spacing
Leveling Buffer Input
Units: seconds (s)
Additional time allocated for precise positioning of lift platforms at floor levels, ensuring safe and accurate alignment for vehicle loading/unloading operations.
Examples: Precision parking lift: 2-3 seconds, Standard lift: 1-2 seconds, Heavy vehicle lift: 3-4 seconds
Loading/Unloading Time Input
Units: seconds (s)
The time required for a vehicle to enter or exit a lift platform, parking space, or service area. Includes driver reaction time and movement until the next movement can occur, being either the lift mechanism or another vehicle commencing entry.
Examples: Car lift entry: 15 seconds
Margin of Error Analysis
Units: varies (± percentage or value)
The desired range of uncertainty in simulation results, typically expressed as ± percentage. Smaller margins require more simulation runs for statistical validity.
Examples: ±0.5% difference between input arrival rate and output arrival rate
Number of Floors Input
Units: integer count
The total number of floors or levels served by a lift, including the ground floor and any non-serviceable levels between other floors. Floors are assumed to be equal height for floor-to-floor spacing.
Examples: 3 storey carpark served by car lift on ground floor
Number of Seeds Input
Units: integer count
The number of independent simulation runs (replications) performed for the specific number of hours, each with a different random pattern of arrivals. More seeds increases reliability of output but leads to longer computation times.
Examples: 15 seeds of 100 hours results in computation of 15 x 100 = 1,500 hours of total simulated operation.
Percentiles Analysis
Units: varies by metric
Statistical measures showing the value below which a certain percentage of observations fall. The 90th percentile means 90% of values are below this threshold and conversely that 10% of values are above.
Examples: 90th percentile queue = 8 cars, 95th percentile wait = 180s, 99th percentile = worst 1% of cases
Queue Length Output
Units: vehicles
The number of vehicles waiting in line at any given time. Often analyzed as percentiles (50th, 90th, 95th) to understand distribution patterns and design for peak demands. Multiply by queue bay length, such as 4.5m per car + 2m between cars = 6.5m bay length, to convert to length in metres.
Examples: 90th percentile: 8 cars and 8 x 6.5 = 52m
RL (Reduced Level) Design
Units: metres (m) or millimetres (mm)
The elevation or height of a point relative to a reference datum (typically sea level or project datum). Used to define vertical positions in road and ramp design.
Examples: RL 125.500 = 125.5m above datum, RL difference = vertical rise/fall, Starting RL: 0.000
Segment Length Design
Units: metres (m)
The longitudinal distance of an individual ramp or road segment with constant grade. Ramps are typically divided into multiple segments with varying grades for design flexibility.
Examples: one-way segment = 20m, ramp segment = 2m
Service Time Input
Units: seconds (s)
The time required to process or serve one vehicle at a control point. Can be fixed (deterministic) or follow a statistical distribution (e.g., exponential) to model variability. The service time includes the full time from entry to the system until exit and can include searching for parking space, parking manoeuvre, parking time, payment of a parking fee and operation of mechanical parking system, but does not include any time in queue.

Simulation Duration Input
Units: hours (hr)
The length of time being analysed in the simulation per seed. For continuous time periods for scenarios, such as two-way passing, this can be 100s or 1000s of hours. For finite scenarios such as school pickup queues, the simulation period could be as little as 0.5 hours.
Examples: Two-way passing: 1000 hours per seed, School Pickup Queue: 1.5 hours per seed.
State Breakdown Output
Units: percentage (%) of time
Analysis showing the percentage of time a system spends in different operational states (idle, loading, moving, waiting, etc.).
Examples: Idle: 25%, Loading: 30%, Moving: 35%, Positioning: 10%
Transition Length Design
Units: metres (m)
The horizontal distance over which a change in gradient occurs, providing a smooth transition between different slope segments for vehicle undercarriage protection.
Examples: Grade change of 12.5% over 2m transition, Parking ramp transitions: 2m typical
Utilisation Rate Output
Units: percentage (%)
The percentage of time that a system or facility is actively serving vehicles or occupied compared to the maximum theoretical capacity.
Examples: Car lift utilisation of 85%
Wait Time Output
Units: seconds (s)
The average time a vehicle spends waiting in queue before being served. Can be calculated per arrival (including those who don't wait) or per queued vehicle only.
Examples: Per arrival: 45s average, Per queued vehicle: 120s average, 90th percentile: 180s
Warm-up Period Analysis
Units: hours (hr) or minutes (min)
Initial simulation time that is excluded from results to allow the system to reach steady-state conditions. Simulations provided assume zero usage at time = 0 which may be an unlikely scenario, though for low simulation durations, warm-up time being excluded from statistics would reliably counteract any accuracy impact.
Examples: Exclude first 10 hours, Run second time with intial utilisation based on the average demand from the first run
Width Design
Units: metres (m)
The lateral width of a ramp, lane, or roadway.
Examples: Single lane ramp: 3.6m, Two-way: 6.1m
